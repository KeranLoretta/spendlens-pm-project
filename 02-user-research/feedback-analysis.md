# User Feedback Analysis

## Research Goal
To understand how users feel about spending visibility in UPI-based payment apps and identify the main pain points that affect budgeting, awareness, and retention.

## Method
We reviewed simulated user feedback, app-style reviews, and behavioral research on UPI spending patterns. The feedback was grouped using affinity mapping to identify repeated themes and root causes.

## Key Themes

### 1. Lack of Spending Visibility
Many users said they can see transactions, but not meaningful summaries of where their money goes. They want monthly totals, category-wise spending, and clearer patterns.

### 2. No Budgeting Support
Users want the ability to set spending limits, compare actual spending against a budget, and receive alerts before they overspend.

### 3. Manual Tracking is Tedious
Several users currently depend on screenshots, notebooks, or Excel sheets to track expenses. This creates friction and leads to inconsistent tracking.

### 4. Weak Comparative Insights
Users want to know whether their spending has increased or decreased compared to previous weeks or months. Without comparison, they cannot evaluate progress.

### 5. Low Real-Time Awareness
UPI makes spending feel effortless, so users often do not realize how much they have spent until later. This reduces financial discipline and increases overspending risk [web:13].

## Core Insight
The app behaves like a transaction ledger, not a financial awareness tool. Users need automatic categorization, summaries, budgeting support, and visual insights to manage spending better.

## Evidence
Research on UPI users found that approximately 75% reported increased spending due to UPI’s intangible nature, and 87.9% of participants said an expense tracker with monthly, category-based, and day-wise views would be useful [web:13].

## Conclusion
The biggest opportunity is to turn UPI apps from simple payment tools into financial awareness tools that help users understand, control, and improve their spending behavior [web:13].
